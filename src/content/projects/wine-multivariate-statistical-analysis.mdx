<Overview>
  Red wine and white wine taste different, but here is the better question: are they different in measurable ways, and which measurements matter most? This project answers that with statistics. The short version: yes, the difference is real, and a small handful of chemistry features carry most of the signal.
</Overview>

## The question

Given the UCI wine-quality dataset — eleven physiochemical measurements plus a quality
rating for ~6,500 Portuguese wines — can we identify which features actually separate
reds from whites, and can we build a classifier good enough to trust?

## Methods

- **Exploratory analysis.** Univariate distributions, pairwise correlations, and
  side-by-side boxplots to spot the obvious differentiators and rule out collinearity
  surprises.
- **MANOVA.** A multivariate test on all eleven features simultaneously, with follow-up
  univariate ANOVAs and Tukey HSDs to isolate which features carry significance.
- **Supervised classification.** Logistic regression, Support Vector Machines, and
  Random Forest, evaluated with 10-fold cross-validation, ROC AUC, and Cohen kappa.
- **Unsupervised clustering.** K-means on standardized features to check whether the
  red/white split shows up without the labels.

## Results

Random Forest reaches roughly **99% classification accuracy** with a kappa of 0.97.
Volatile acidity, total sulfur dioxide, and chlorides do most of the lifting — they
alone separate the classes with high accuracy. K-means on unlabeled data recovers the
red/white split as the dominant axis of variation.

The supervised lift over a naive baseline is large, but the more useful finding is
methodological: a careful MANOVA + ANOVA path gives you the same conclusion as the ML
pipeline, with far more interpretability.

## Takeaways

- **Multivariate first, univariate second.** Running univariate tests on every feature
  blindly inflates the false-positive rate; MANOVA is the right entry point.
- **Don't oversell the ML.** A linear model on the three top features gets you most of
  the way there. The 99% Random Forest accuracy is a sanity check, not the headline.
- **Interpretability is a deliverable.** Volatile acidity being the strongest separator
  is a more useful conclusion than a confusion-matrix screenshot.
